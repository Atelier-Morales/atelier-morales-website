Bohios

Prague Biennale, June - August 2003

Teresa Ayuso and Juan Luis Morales Menocal make up the collective Atelier Morales, which was recently featured in the special edition Teamwork in Exit magazine (Madrid, August-October 2002). Both Cuban architects, they left their country ten years ago and arrived in Paris, where they formed Atelier Morales in 1993. Since then, the collective has exhibited their multi-media works, which have always focused on homage to their Cuban heritage. Projects for restaurants in Paris, Strasbourg, Caen, Chartres and other cities in France include the designs for facades, furniture, and murals. They have also produced paintings, collages, and drawings.

For the Armory Show 2003, New York, Atelier Morales created "Bohios", a project interwoven with photography, painting, collage, investigation on site, selection, classification, and exhibition which includes objects, elements, and music that form part of an abandoned cultural patrimony.

Bohios, which will be shown at the Prague Biennial 2003, is the name given to the Guajiros (peasant) homes in Cuba ever since the Taino indigenous people first appeared in the island centuries ago. The bohios are huts made of Opencast, or hard, fleshy leafs of the Palma Real trees. They have earth floors and guano (palm leaf) ceilings. With time, the peasants have modified the bohio, adding wood boards or even cement blocks, bricks, and terracotta to the walls and ceiling. The bohios are rapidly losing their charm and natural beauty. Some of the original bohios are still scattered around the island, but many have been destroyed by man (specially after the 1959 Revolution) as well as by hurricanes.

The bohios are disappearing in the face of indifference and apathy. New generations are forgetting the popular traditions of construction that have been transmitted from one generation to the next. Poor and richer guajiros are either Modernizing or demolishing the bohio - now in the process of extinction.

This series is about "reveries" and "recuperations" and just as patrimony is lost, recovery is always a possibility through art and culture. The images become a contemporary dialogue between artists and spectators that salvages in the manner of a laboratory the modus vivendis and abstract poetry that surround the Cuban peasantry. This is why some of the works of Atelier Morales are dated in the future, 2010, for example. Ayuso-Morales Menocal believe that, in years to come, the bohios will still be there, transforming themselves slowly while faced with the prospect of extinction.
