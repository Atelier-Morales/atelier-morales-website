LOS BALNEARIOS (The baths)

Los Balnearios (“The Baths”) is part of the body of work that Atelier Morales (Juan Luis Morales and Teresa Ayuso) have titled “Patrimonio a la deriva” (Patrimony Adrift). The first series of Patrimony Adrift talked about the steady disappearance of the Bohíos (Cuban countryside huts as build by the Taino Indian tradition); the second, “The Sugar Mills”, refers to the decline of the sugar industry in Cuba; then the third series which now concerns us is Los Balnearios, which reflects upon the loss of the Cuban baths as small, recreational and therapeutical havens to give place to massive and impersonal tourist complexes.

In all three series Atelier Morales have followed the footsteps of French voyageurs that traveled to Cuba 150 years ago. Then Juan Luis and Teresa revisited the scenarios described by the Frenchmen in books, paintings or engravings and took pictures of these places in their actual, desolate states.

Los Balnearios is a series of 25 lenticular digital photographs of abandoned baths mainly in Matanzas, Pinar del Rio and Havana,that the artists offer as homage to French lithographer Frederic Mialhe, known for his views of Cuba published in the Album La Isla de Cuba Pintoresca in 1838. Mialhe traveled throughout Cuba from 1830 to 1836, finding dream-like places with rare qualities for visitors; he did drawings, paintings, photographs and engravings of landscapes that were later printed and distributed as sorts of “postal cards”. The landscapes became the first papers that credited Cuba as a land with attractions for tourism.

Cuban baths were built in the 19th and 20th century in many of the places depicted by Mialhe. They were baths with regional charm, built for pleasure and curative purposes. In the early 1930s beach houses appeared as clubs and resorts, but always with a provincial allure. It wasn’t until after the Cuban Revolution that these places were nationalized; later abandoned they have altogether died. They are falling apart in pieces; some have been ruins for many years already.

In their works “Los Balnearios” Atelier Morales show the old baths and resorts as they are now in agony. Sculptures of angels and figures found in the cemeteries of the world are incorporated in all their pictures, creating a dialogue with space and the lost patrimony. Death has taken over to give lieu to the gross and global tourism industry.

Nina Menocal, 2007
